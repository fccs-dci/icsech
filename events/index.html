In recent years, Food Studies has emerged as a dynamic interdisciplinary field, blending insights from multiple disciplines in exploring the pivotal role of food in shaping societies. The upcoming CCS webinar offers an opportunity to delve into how food culture has both historically and contemporarily operated in China. This panel will bring together two scholars to discuss their research projects into the significance of food in Chinese religious and social practices. By examining food as a lens to understand broader cultural and social transformations, this webinar promises to contribute new perspectives to the growing discourse on China’s vibrant food culture. Dr. Feng's presentation, “'Eat Bitterness,' Process Trauma: Two Novels by Wang Anyi and Ge Liang," argues that the two novels affirm the life-sustaining efficacy of food that defies the gravity of inherited traumas in twentieth- and twenty-first-century Greater China. Dr. DuBois's presentation, "Exploring Taste in Chinese Food Writing" will examine the Chinese word for "taste" (wei 味) spans a range of distinct, but related ideas. His talk will use the idea of taste in Chinese food writing to explore the key relationship between the foods you crave and the ones you need.

But what of artists whose lives were fundamentally altered by conflicts of the twentieth century, yet who chose to never directly represent their traumatic experiences?Solace in Paintingexplores how we characterize the artwork of conflicted artists of the Asian diaspora who never produced overt “conflict art.” The first national traveling exhibition organized by UTA, the show features three little-researched diasporic painters—Chao Shao-an (1905-1998), Keisho Okayama (1934- 2018), and Ann Phong (1957-)—brought together through their unusual approaches to painting shared experiences of major conflicts in Asia. PROF. CHANG CHUEN MEMORIAL PUBLIC LECTURE SERIES (2024-25) - THE INVENTION OF ZHANG BAOZAI (張保仔), THE PIRATE

PROF. ROBERT ANTONY

March 5, 2025 at 1:00 AM UTC

Undoubtedly Zhang Baozai 張保仔 is the most famous, or notorious, Cantonese pirate in Chinese history. Everyone in Hong Kong and Macau seems to know some story about him. But for all that has been written and said about him, the fact is we know very little about the real Zhang Baozai. What we know for sure is that he was a pirate leader in the early nineteenth century and that he surrendered to the Qing government in 1810. Afterward he served in the Qing military and died relatively young in 1822. The earliest documentary evidence that mentions Zhang Baozai is in a Palace Memorial (奏摺) dated 1808. But the first important, and extensive, information on him only appeared in 1830 in a Chinese book entitled The Pacification of the Seas (靖海氛記) by Yuan Yonglun; a year later this book was translated into English. With these books, I argue, we see the start of the “invention” or “cultural construction” of Zhang Baozai. After 1830 more and more stories began to appear in print and orally handed down from generation to generation. In this presentation I discuss how the pirate Zhang Baozai was “invented” as a hero and even as a deity. I examine legends and stories collected from Macau, Hong Kong, and the Canton Delta, as well as historical “facts” in archives and other historical texts, surrounding his life and deeds. While many writers and scholars today have dismissed legends as unimportant, I argue to the contrary that they are important historical and cultural sources, especially for understanding the popular culture and mentality of ordinary people.
