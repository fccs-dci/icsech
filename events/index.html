W22 NOON LECTURE SERIES ~ GLITCHES IN ART HISTORICAL FLOW, CA. 1750

&NBSP; MICHELE MATTEINI, ASSISTANT PROFESSOR OF ART HISTORY, NEW YORK UNIVERSITY

February 22, 2022, 5:00 PM UTC

&nbsp; The history of ink painting in early modern China is often told as a history of uninterrupted lineages and seamless transmission through time. There were, however, passages in that history when transmission was not so certain, and artists developed modes of painting that put under pressure teachings and standards inherited from the past. This was the case of Zheng Xie (1693-1765) whose monochrome ink orchids were conceived as a string of tiny but effective disruptions of the technical and aesthetic principles of monochrome ink painting. Focusing on Zheng Xie’s late production, this talk explores what glitches, errors, and flaws tell us about mid-Qing artists’ attitudes toward the legacy of the past and the value they assigned to engage with a crumbling world. Michele Matteini is Assistant Professor at New York University. His book, "A Ghost in the City: Luo Ping and the Craft of Painting in Eighteenth-Century Beijing" is forthcoming later this year.

MIGRATION AND POPULAR RESISTANCE IN RURAL CHINA

LU YAO (COLUMBIA UNIVERSITY)

February 23, 2022, 1:30 AM UTC

Massive rural-urban migration and growing collective resistance are two profound transformations in China. How are these two phenomena connected? Also, rural and urban protests in China have been portrayed as fragmented phenomena. With greater geographic mobility, have the urban-rural linkages been restored to a certain extent? Drawing on several data sources, I study the effects of migration on collective action in rural China. I find that migration constitutes a politically transformative experience for many migrants. In turn, migrants act as a vehicle of political diffusion for their sending communities. The role of migration can be understood in the context of distinct institutional arrangements in China, which were originally engineered to disenfranchise rural-origin people but which instead have inadvertently politicized migrants and peasants alike.

MICRO-INSTITUTIONAL FOUNDATIONS OF CAPITALISM: SECTORAL PATHWAYS TO GLOBALIZATION IN CHINA, INDIA, AND RUSSIA

ROSELYN HSUEH (TEMPLE UNIVERSITY)

February 24, 2022, 12:30 AM UTC

What is the relationship between internal development and integration into the global economy in developing countries? How do state-market relations differ? And do these differences matter in the post-Cold War era of global conflict and cooperation? Drawing on research in China, India, and Russia, Micro-Institutional Foundations of Capitalism introduces a new theory of sectoral pathways to globalization and development. Adopting a historical and comparative approach, Hsueh's framework shows how state elites perceive the strategic value of sectors in response to internal and external pressures. The resultant dominant patterns of market governance vary by country and sector within country. These national configurations of sectoral models are the micro-institutional foundations of capitalism, which mediate globalization and development.

CHINA HUMANITIES SEMINAR FEATURING YUHANG LI – ENGINEERING RELIGIOUS BLISS AT THE QING COURT: JILE SHIJIE IN THE BEIHAI PARK

YUHANG LI,&NBSP;UNIVERSITY OF WISCONSIN-MADISON

February 28, 2022, 10:00 PM UTC

In 1770, with the purpose of presenting an unusual surprising gift to his mother Empress Dowager Chongqing (1692-1777) for her eightieth birthday, Emperor Qianlong (1711-1799) ordered the imperial architectural department to construct a Buddhist compound named&nbsp;jile shijie&nbsp;or blissful land on the northern shore of imperial Beihai Park next to the Forbidden City. Inside of the main hall, instead of conventional Buddhist icons staged on the lotus pedestals, an innovative three-dimensional clay mountain site scenery adorned with various deities from the Pure Land occupies the interior space.&nbsp;Jile&nbsp;shijie, a synonym for the Western Paradise and Pure Land, has been consistently visualized and contemplated since early medieval China. But the&nbsp;jile shijie&nbsp;built for Empress Dowager Chongqing is a standalone case which creates the experience of religious joy through a site scenery.&nbsp;The Pure Land is usually experienced as a future connected to death, which one literally cannot experience as present.&nbsp; However, Qianlong’s filial gift allows his mother to feel the required affect in this world, by juxtaposing transcendence and immanence.&nbsp; The absolute future of the Pure Land, a future that one experiences only after one has no more future on earth, becomes present at least in part, in a man-made small-scale western paradise. In this paper, I will discuss the surviving architecture, sculptural mountain preserved in old photographs, imperial documents on the design process, and Qianlong’s own writings on the given subject. Through unpacking the layers of this site, I will demonstrate how a liminal temporality of religious joy is materialized.

POST-URBANISM 2.0: INFRASTRUCTURE SPACES AND HALF-FINISHED CITIES IN CHINA

TIM OAKES (UNIVERSITY OF COLORADO BOULDER)

March 2, 2022, 1:30 AM UTC

This talk explores China’s National New Area experimental urban development zones as ‘infrastructure spaces’ of diffuse and fragmented urbanization. Such spaces typically feature a rapidly built, large scale infrastructure grid laid upon an existing though increasingly obsolete rural landscape. They also seem to suggest a new model of urban development that deliberately eschews agglomeration and existing urban nodes. While rural residents struggle to reinvent their lives in preparation for the promised city-to-come, the inherent incompleteness of the infrastructure space introduces a state of suspension that envelops everyday life in the New Area. The central role of transportation infrastructures, and the logics of suspension and circulation that obtain in these spaces, provide echoes of historical Chinese and Asian patterns of urbanization in which the neat and tidy distinctions between rural and urban that underlie conventional (Western) urban theory have never materialized.

GREEN GOLD AND PAPER GOLD: FINANCING THE CHINESE-AMERICAN TEA TRADE BEFORE THE OPIUM WAR

DR. DAN DU (UNIVERSITY OF NORTH CAROLINA AT CHARLOTTE)

March 5, 2022, 1:00 AM UTC

This research explores the credit economy of the tea trade between China and the United States from 1784 – 1842. Preoccupied with the large volume of silver flowing into China, previous research portrayed China’s foreign trade as a cumbersome system undergirded primarily by a barter economy and silver payments. This research, however, spotlights cashless payments in China, particularly promissory notes and bills of exchange which were conventionally considered as financial devices of Western capitalism. These credit instruments had commonly facilitated de facto transactions between Chinese and U.S. merchants in the tea trade. Moreover, Chinese traders loaned millions of dollars to their American counterparts through promissory notes, thus lubricating the money-scarce early U.S. economy. Through the flow of tea and opium, bills of exchange produced an active financial market in China, which eventually triggered the Panic of 1837 in the United States and the First Opium War in China.

SHADOW BANKING AND STATE FINANCES IN CHINA

March 9, 2022, 8:00 PM UTC

The fundamental reason for the extreme leverage today in China’s banks, enterprises and the state itself is found in the decentralized fiscal arrangements of highly self-reliant local governments. This problem has been compounded by the excessive stimulus lending over the past decade. As a result Beijing has promoted the creation of an extensive shadow banking system designed to protect the stability of the major state banks. Stepping back this has led to the state’s growing leverage. This presentation focuses on the impact of the shadow banking system on the state’s finances and compares the costs of China’s response to the global financial crisis with the US response.

W22 LRCCS NOON LECTURE SERIES ~ THE MAKING OF “GOOD” CITIZENS: EXAMINING THE MECHANISM OF AND PUBLIC SUPPORT FOR CHINA’S SOCIAL CREDIT SYSTEM

&NBSP; FAN LIANG, ASSISTANT PROFESSOR OF MEDIA, DUKE KUNSHAN UNIVERSITY

April 12, 2022, 4:00 PM UTC

&nbsp; This talk examines citizen scoring in and public support for China’s Social Credit System (SCS). Dr. Fan analyzed the mechanism of credit scoring embedded in the SCS and show two facets of the SCS: a normative apparatus fostering “good” citizens and a regulative apparatus normalizing “deviant” behaviors. He also used a survey experiment to test how types of monitored behaviors and different information exposure explain public support for the SCS. EXAMINING THE MULTI-DIMENSIONAL AND MULTISCALAR HEALTHCARE INEQUALITIES IN URBAN CHINA: TOWARDS A SYNTHESISED APPROACH

SHENJING HE (THE UNIVERSITY OF HONG KONG) AND XIANG YAN (THE UNIVERSITY OF HONG KONG)

November 3, 2022, 12:30 AM UTC

Public health scholars and geographers examine healthcare inequalities using different approaches, leaving salient research gaps due to disciplinary divide. Through a presentation of three studies, we develop a synthesized approach to understand the multi-dimensional and multiscalar healthcare inequalities in urban China through combining the strengths of both public health and geography. The first study examines multi-scalar unevenness in the distribution of China’s high-quality healthcare resources and the underlying mechanisms. The second study investigates cross-city patient mobility in China and its impacts on healthcare equity and efficiency. The third study explores the divergent distributions of physicians and healthcare beds in China and the determinants. These three studies present a synthesized approach underpinned by an in-depth engagement with the complex healthcare system through geographical lenses. To conclude, we propose several ways to break disciplinary silos.
