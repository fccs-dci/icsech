13:Tc7e,<p>Nora should not step out and find her own way. She should stay at home, dress modestly, and be a good wife. Chinese silent films of the 1920s and early 1930s often convey ideological messages strikingly at odds with May Fourth ideals such as equality of the sexes and liberation of the individual. While some films express sympathy for progressive causes and outrage at present inequalities, others are overtly misogynistic and reactionary. This talk will focus on Chinese-made films that May Fourth figures would consider to be on the wrong side of history, and show how they harness the power of the film medium—charismatic stars, special effects, popular Hollywood tropes—to take us back to the good old days. On this May Fourth anniversary, bring your sense of righteousness and film studies sensibilities to this tour through moral dramas such as&nbsp;<em>The Pearl Necklace</em>&nbsp;一串珍珠 (1926),&nbsp;<em>Don’t Change Your Husband</em>&nbsp;情海重吻 (1929),&nbsp;<em>Poor Daddy</em>&nbsp;怕老婆 (1929),&nbsp;<em>Love and Duty</em>&nbsp;戀愛與義務 (1931), and&nbsp;<em>The Peach Girl</em>&nbsp;桃花泣血記 (1931). #<em>NoraStayHome</em></p>

<p><strong>Christopher Rea</strong>&nbsp;is Professor of Chinese and former Director of the Centre for Chinese Research at the University of British Columbia. He is the creator of the Chinese Film Classics Project, whose website&nbsp;<a href="https://chinesefilmclassics.org/" rel="nofollow noopener noreferrer" target="_blank">ChineseFilmClassics.org</a>&nbsp;hosts over 30 early Chinese films translated by Dr Rea and collaborators, as well as film clips, essays, links, and a free online course on early Chinese films. The website and the course are companions to his book&nbsp;<a href="https://cup.columbia.edu/book/chinese-film-classics-19221949/9780231188135" rel="nofollow noopener noreferrer" target="_blank"><em>Chinese Film Classics, 1922-1949</em></a>&nbsp;(Columbia, 2021), which covers fourteen films, and has a Chinese edition forthcoming. Full films and over 200 film clips (songs, gags, superlative scenes) are also viewable at the&nbsp;<a href="https://www.youtube.com/@ModernChineseCulturalStudies" rel="nofollow noopener noreferrer" target="_blank">YouTube channel&nbsp;</a>@ModernChineseCulturalStudies. Rea is also the author of&nbsp;<a href="https://www.ucpress.edu/book/9780520283848/the-age-of-irreverence" rel="nofollow noopener noreferrer" target="_blank"><em>The Age of Irreverence: A New History of Laughter in China</em></a>&nbsp;(California, 2015) and the co-author of&nbsp;<a href="https://press.uchicago.edu/ucp/books/book/chicago/W/bo131341275.html" rel="nofollow noopener noreferrer" target="_blank"><em>Where Research Begins: Choosing a Research Project That Matters to You</em></a>&nbsp;(and the World) (with Thomas Mullaney; Chicago, 2022). He was a postdoctoral fellow at the Australian Centre on China in the World in 2012.</p>
