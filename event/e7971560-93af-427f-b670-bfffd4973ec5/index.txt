13:T237f,<p>Newspaper headlines describe a China that is uniformly bleak: a slowing economy, tensions with the West, and a surveillance state that seems to have crushed all opposing voices. But in his new book&nbsp;<i>Sparks: China’s Underground Historians and Their Battle for the Future</i>&nbsp;(2023), Ian Johnson presents a more nuanced picture of Xi Jinping’s China, one where a vibrant movement of underground filmmakers,&nbsp;&nbsp;magazine publishers, and authors challenge the Communist Party on its most important&nbsp;source of legitimacy: its control of history.</p>

<p>Join us as Johnson presents his book and introduces us to China’s counter-history movement, which echoes struggles in our own countries for a more equitable and just accounting of the past.</p>

<p><strong>About the Speaker:&nbsp;</strong></p>

<p><strong>Ian Johnson&nbsp;</strong>is a Pulitzer Prize-winning writer, researcher, and&nbsp;<a href="https://www.cfr.org/news-releases/cfr-welcome-new-fellows-china-india-and-defense-policy" rel="noopener noreferrer" target="_blank">senior fellow for China studies</a>&nbsp;at the Council on Foreign Relations. His new book,&nbsp;<a href="http://tgc.ca8.myftpupload.com/sparks-chinas-underground-historians/"><em>Sparks: China’s Underground Historians and their Battle for the Future</em></a>, shows how–despite the best efforts of Xi Jinping’s surveillance state–a nationwide movement has coalesced to challenge the Communist Party on its most hallowed ground: its control of history.</p>

<section data-element_type="section" data-id="0beaff8" data-settings="{&quot;animation&quot;:&quot;fadeIn&quot;}">
<p>Johnson has lived in China for more than 20 years and has been following the country since first going there&nbsp;<a href="https://www.chinafile.com/dengs-heyday" rel="noopener noreferrer" target="_blank">as a student in Beijing</a>&nbsp;from 1984 to 1985. He also studied Chinese&nbsp;<a href="http://mtc.ntnu.edu.tw/mtcweb/index.php?lang=en" rel="noopener noreferrer" target="_blank">in Taipei&nbsp;</a>from 1986 to 1988, translating articles from mainland newspapers for a mainland monitoring service and traveling widely around the country.</p>

<p>He worked as a newspaper correspondent in China from 1994 to 1996 with Baltimore’s&nbsp;<em>The Sun,</em>&nbsp;and from 1997 to 2001 with&nbsp;<em>The Wall Street Journal</em>, where he covered macro economics, China’s WTO accession and social issues, such as the rise of popular religion and the Falun Gong crackdown<em>.&nbsp;</em>During this time also volunteered for a U.S. registered charity, The&nbsp;<a href="https://greatnonprofits.org/org/the-taoist-restoration-society-inc" rel="noopener noreferrer" target="_blank">Taoist Restoration Society</a>, which brought him into close contact with China’s only indigenous religion.</p>

<p>In 2009, Johnson returned to China, living there until 2020 when he was&nbsp;<a href="https://www.nytimes.com/2020/07/16/opinion/sunday/china-us-cold-war.html" rel="noopener noreferrer" target="_blank">expelled from China</a>&nbsp;as part of worsening tensions between China and the United States. He wrote regularly for&nbsp;<a href="https://www.nytimes.com/by/ian-johnson" rel="noopener noreferrer" target="_blank"><em>The New York Times</em></a><em>,&nbsp;</em><a href="http://www.nybooks.com/contributors/ian-johnson/" rel="noopener noreferrer" target="_blank"><em>The New York Review of Books</em></a><em>,&nbsp;</em>and other publications. He taught undergraduates<a href="http://www.thebeijingcenter.org/people/ian-johnson/" rel="noopener noreferrer" target="_blank">&nbsp;at The Beijing Center for Chinese Studies</a>, and&nbsp;served as an advisor to<strong><em>&nbsp;</em></strong><a href="https://www.cambridge.org/core/journals/journal-of-asian-studies" rel="noopener noreferrer" target="_blank"><em>Th</em><em>e&nbsp;Journal of Asian Studies</em></a><strong><em>.</em></strong>&nbsp;He is currently a senior fellow for China studies at the Council on Foreign Relations in New York, and is&nbsp;<a href="https://www.gko.uni-leipzig.de/ostasiatisches-institut/sinologie/forschung/laufende-promotionen/" rel="noopener noreferrer" target="_blank">pursuing a PhD at the University of Leipzig</a>&nbsp;on Chinese religious associations.</p>

<p>He has worked in Germany twice. From 1988 to 1992 he attended graduate school in West Berlin and covered the fall of the Berlin Wall and German unification. In 2001 he moved back to Berlin, working until 2009 as&nbsp;<em>The Wall Street Journal</em>‘s Germany bureau chief and senior writer.</p>

<p>He managed reporters covering EU fiscal policy and macro-economics, and wrote about social issues such as Islamist terrorism.</p>
</section>

<section data-element_type="section" data-id="3dc688e" data-settings="{&quot;animation&quot;:&quot;fadeIn&quot;}">
<p>Johnson&nbsp;<a href="https://www.pulitzer.org/winners/ian-johnson" rel="noopener noreferrer" target="_blank">won the 2001 Pulitzer Prize&nbsp;for his coverage of China</a>, two awards from the Overseas Press Club, an award from&nbsp;<strong>the&nbsp;</strong><a href="https://www.spj.org/sdxa2000.asp" rel="noopener noreferrer" target="_blank">Society of Professional Journalists</a>, and&nbsp;<a href="http://aparc.fsi.stanford.edu/news/ian-johnson-longtime-foreign-correspondent-receive-shorenstein-journalism-award" rel="noopener noreferrer" target="_blank">Stanford University’s Shorenstein Journalism Award</a><strong>&nbsp;</strong>for his body of work covering Asia. In 2019 he won the American Academy of Religion’s&nbsp;<a href="https://www.aarweb.org/AARMBR/Who-We-Are-/Award-Programs-/Awards/Journalism-Award-Winners-and-Sample-Articles.aspx" rel="noopener noreferrer" target="_blank">“best in-depth newswriting” award</a>.</p>

<p>In 2006-07 he spent a year as&nbsp;<a href="https://niemanreports.org/news/2006/05/nieman-foundation-announces-u-s-and-international-fellows-for-2006-2007/" rel="noopener noreferrer" target="_blank">a Nieman fellow</a>&nbsp;at Harvard University, and later received research and writing grants from&nbsp;<a href="https://www.opensocietyfoundations.org/grants/open-society-fellowship?fellow=ian-johnson" rel="noopener noreferrer" target="_blank">the Open Society Foundation</a><strong>,</strong>&nbsp;the&nbsp;<a href="http://pulitzercenter.org/people/ian-johnson" rel="noopener noreferrer" target="_blank">Pulitzer Center on Crisis Reporting</a><strong>,</strong>&nbsp;and the&nbsp;<a href="https://aliciapatterson.org/users/ian-johnson" rel="noopener noreferrer" target="_blank">Alicia Patterson Foundation</a>. In 2020, he was an&nbsp;<a href="https://web.archive.org/web/20211008112123/https://silversfoundation.org/2019-grantees/" rel="noopener noreferrer" target="_blank">inaugural grantee</a>&nbsp;of the Robert B. Silvers Foundation for work-in-progress. He was also awarded&nbsp;<a href="http://tgc.ca8.myftpupload.com/neh-public-scholar/" rel="noopener noreferrer" target="_blank">a&nbsp;2020-2021 National Endowment for the Humanities Public Scholars&nbsp;fellowship</a>&nbsp;for a new book he is writing on China’s unofficial history.</p>

<p>Johnson&nbsp;<a href="http://tgc.ca8.myftpupload.com/books/">has published four books</a>&nbsp;and contributed chapters to four others. In addition to&nbsp;<em>Sparks</em>, his other books explore China’s religious revival and its political implications (<a href="https://ian-johnson.com/the-souls-of-china" rel="noopener noreferrer" target="_blank"><em>T</em><em>he Souls of China: The Return of Religion After Mao</em></a><em><strong>, 2017),&nbsp;</strong></em>civil society and grassroots protest in China (<a href="https://ian-johnson.com/wild-grass" rel="noopener noreferrer" target="_blank"><em>Wild Grass</em></a><em>,</em>&nbsp;2004) and Islamism and the Cold War in Europe (<a href="https://ian-johnson.com/a-mosque-in-munich" rel="noopener noreferrer" target="_blank"><em>A Mosque in Munich</em></a><em>,</em>&nbsp;2010).</p>

<p>He has also contributed chapters to<strong>:&nbsp;</strong><a href="https://ian-johnson.com/my-first-trip-to-china" rel="noopener noreferrer" target="_blank"><em>My First Trip to China</em><em>&nbsp;</em></a>(2011),&nbsp;<a href="https://ian-johnson.com/chinese-characters" rel="noopener noreferrer" target="_blank"><em>Chinese Character</em><em>s&nbsp;</em></a>(2012), the<strong>&nbsp;</strong><a href="https://global.oup.com/academic/product/the-oxford-illustrated-history-of-modern-china-9780199683758?q=illustrated%20history%20of%20china&amp;lang=en&amp;cc=us" rel="noopener noreferrer" target="_blank"><em>Oxford Illustrated History of Modern China</em></a>(2016), and wrote a 5,000-word introduction to&nbsp;<a href="https://ian-johnson.com/forbidden-city" rel="noopener noreferrer" target="_blank"><em>The Forbidden City: The Palace at the Heart of Chinese Culture</em></a>&nbsp;(2021).</p>

<p>Johnson was born in Montréal, Canada. He holds U.S. and Canadian citizenship. He lives with his family in Brooklyn, New York.</p>
