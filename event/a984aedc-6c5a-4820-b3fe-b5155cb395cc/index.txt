17:Tb5b,<p style="text-indent:.5in"><span style="font-size:12pt"><span style="line-height:150%"><span style="font-family:Calibri,sans-serif"><span style="font-family:&quot;Times New Roman&quot;,serif">This presentation draws on a 5-year ethnographic study of the social and subjective experiences of fifty young women from China through the years of their university study in Australia, whose findings were recently published in <i>Dreams of Flight: The Lives of Chinese Women Students in the West</i> (Duke U.P. 2022). </span></span></span></span></p>

<p style="text-indent:.5in"><span style="font-size:12pt"><span style="line-height:150%"><span style="font-family:Calibri,sans-serif"><span style="font-family:&quot;Times New Roman&quot;,serif">Although around 60% of outgoing students from China are female, the gendered dimensions of China’s educational diaspora have to date been little discussed. My study was interested in how educational mobility affects post-90s middle-class women’s negotiations of the contradictory gendered life scripts and self-understandings available to them today. One of the study’s premises was the observation of a tension in contemporary Chinese public culture between, on the one hand, a neoliberal-style discourse of self-reliant, self-entrepreneurial professional subjecthood that has strong interpellative power for well-resourced, middle-class, urban singleton daughters; and, on the other hand, a neotraditionalist discourse of women’s inherently family-centred “nature” and their biological destiny to marry and have a child(ren) by age thirty. </span></span></span></span></p>

<p style="text-indent:.5in"><span style="font-size:12pt"><span style="line-height:150%"><span style="font-family:Calibri,sans-serif"><span style="font-family:&quot;Times New Roman&quot;,serif">The research reveals an overseas-graduate subjectivity marked by decreasing identification with the neotraditionalist model of femininity, and correspondingly increased identification with mobile enterprising selfhood. Broadly, graduates emerged as market subjects: a willingly mobile labour force suited in terms of ideological orientation and self-understanding, as well as concrete skills, to work in the engine rooms of global capitalism as financiers, professionals, and high-level technicians. This broad tendency, however, is attended by multiple complexities connected with the women’s situations vis-à-vis class, gender, family, work, and mobility itself, which I will explore in this presentation through selected participant stories.</span></span></span></span></p>
