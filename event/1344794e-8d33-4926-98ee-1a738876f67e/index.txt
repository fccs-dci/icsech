13:Tca6,<p>This lecture explores the life of an elite woman, raised in Beijing and educated at a missionary school during the May Fourth era. Daughter of a Beiyang reformer, wife of a Nationalist official educated at MIT, mother of an underground Communist revolutionary, she herself left no direct trace in the public record. She emerges primarily in interviews with her son, and in an unfinished and unpublished historical novel by her daughter. <p>Speaker:</p>

<p><strong>Gail Hershatter</strong>, Research Professor and Distinguished Professor Emer. of History at the University of California, Santa Cruz.&nbsp;</p>

<p>Professor Hershatter is a former President of the Association for Asian Studies. Her works include&nbsp;<em>The Workers of Tianjin&nbsp;</em>(1986, Chinese translation 2016),&nbsp;<em>Personal Voices: China Women in the 1980s&nbsp;</em>(1988, with Emily Honig),&nbsp;<em>Dangerous Pleasures: Prostitution in Twentieth-Century Shanghai&nbsp;</em>(1997, Chinese translation 2003),&nbsp;<em>Women in China’s Long Twentieth Century</em>&nbsp;(2004),&nbsp;<em>The Gender of Memory: Rural Women and China’s Collective Past&nbsp;</em>(2011; Chinese translation 2017) and&nbsp;<em>Women and China’s Revolutions&nbsp;</em>(2019).&nbsp;</p>

<p>Moderated by&nbsp;<strong>JM Chris Chang</strong>,&nbsp;&nbsp;Asia in Action / Public Humanities Postdoctoral Fellow at the Weatherhead East Asian Institute.&nbsp;</p>

<p>This event is the second in a four-part series for WEAI's Asia in Action workshop series, "Remapping the Archives: New Histories of the PRC."</p>

<p>Series description:</p>

<p>In the present moment, intensifying historical censorship in China–compounded by the lasting impacts of the pandemic–has severed access to the archive as we once knew it. The Modern China field has been forced to reckon with the possibility that access to PRC sources may soon become exceptional, and that its foreclosure portends a post-archival future. What is history without archive, or archive without history? The purpose of this four-part series is to explore promising responses by several scholars to this crisis in the archive, both as a means of illuminating new methodological directions in PRC history as well as reexamining perennial historical questions from a new aspect.&nbsp;</p>
