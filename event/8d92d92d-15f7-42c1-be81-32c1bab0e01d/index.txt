13:T989,<p><span style="font-size:12pt"><span style="font-family:&quot;Times New Roman&quot;,serif"><span style="font-size:10.0pt"><span style="font-family:&quot;Calibri&quot;,sans-serif">The present talk addresses the status of Wang Bi </span></span><span lang="ZH-CN" style="font-size:10.0pt"><span style="font-family:SimSun">王弼</span></span><span style="font-size:10.0pt"><span style="font-family:&quot;Calibri&quot;,sans-serif"> (226‒249)’s <i>Commentary on the </i>Book of Changes (<i>Zhouyi Zhu </i></span></span><span lang="ZH-CN" style="font-size:10.0pt"><span style="font-family:SimSun">周易注</span></span><span style="font-size:10.0pt"><span style="font-family:&quot;Calibri&quot;,sans-serif">) in the overall framework of his thought. Apart from a few exceptions, Wang Bi’s thought has been approached through his <i>Commentary on the </i>Laozi (<i>Laozi Zhu </i></span></span><span lang="ZH-CN" style="font-size:10.0pt"><span style="font-family:SimSun">老子注</span></span><span style="font-size:10.0pt"><span style="font-family:&quot;Calibri&quot;,sans-serif">). In this perspective, the two commentaries are either read as sharing the same philosophical approach and as pertaining to the same theoretical endeavour (this is notably R. Wagner’s approach<a name="_Int_3GiNaCEs">), or</a> studied apart from each other. In this latter approach, the tone of the <i>Commentary on the </i>Changes, which display a much lesser focus on metaphysical speculations than the <i>Commentary on the </i>Laozi, is interpreted as a sign that the inquiry of this work is almost exclusively turned toward human affairs and political philosophy.&nbsp; This talk demonstrates that another reading is possible. To nuance the idea that Wang Bi’s view on the <i>Changes </i>ultimately focuses on political philosophy, it will be reminded that since the early <i>Yi</i>-tradition, the <i>Changes </i>has been conceived as an all-encompassing book, capable of comprehending the whole cosmos. The speaker will then address the issue of the relationship of Wang Bi’s two commentaries. By doing so, he intends to show how the two works might be related, although their scopes remain distinct. Finally, he will question Wang Bi’s interpretation of the two first hexagrams and stress how his reading establishes the <i>Changes </i>as a book concerned with what is between Heaven and Earth.</span></span></span></span></p>
